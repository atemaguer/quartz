title: "Learn By Doing: How Theory Emerges from Practice"

The canonical pedagogical style in school is to first teach the theory about a subject before then applying the concepts to concrete case examples. At least, this is how most STEM subjects like mathematics and physics are taught. Accordingly, students adopt this style of learning and use it for the rest of their academic and professional careers.

Theory-oriented learning is best suited for settings where a learner has access to a more experienced teacher or tutor who can guide them through the subject. However, it starts to break down in situations where the student is self-teaching.

The issue with a theory-first approach for self-learners is twofold. First, it encourages knowledge consumption over application—knowledge acquired simply from consumption and not applied is often less retained and subsequently lost with time. Meanwhile, knowledge acquired from doing the thing or solving the problem is much stickier and persists for longer. Second, without direction from a teacher or tutor, it's difficult to prioritize which concepts to learn and build a solid foundation for understanding deeper and more complex principles. This often leads to shaky and misconceived knowledge.

A more effective strategy for self-taught settings is to adopt an applied-first approach that encourages doing and practice over consumption.

So what does an applied-first approach look like? Ideally, it starts with learning through practice—through a concrete problem or project. As a self-learner, you want to start learning a given skill or subject by choosing a specific, concrete project that applies that skill or subject knowledge. Build the project or solve the specific problem, only focusing on knowledge that helps you solve that particular problem and learning new knowledge on demand. Repeat this process for another two or three projects or problems that apply the knowledge or theory of the given subject or skill.

After a number of projects or problems, patterns and principles underlying the theory will start to emerge. The principles, when organized and described in detail, become abstract concepts—theory. The abstract concepts can then be applied to novel problems with similar characteristics. Once again, these new problems will uncover new patterns that add to the theory or knowledge of the subject or skill, expanding it.

Repeating this iterative process—solving a specific set of problems, extracting the common patterns across them, uncovering the abstract concepts underpinning them, and then applying this knowledge to new problems—is how one goes from amateur to master in a skill or subject. Don't get me wrong, theory is useful and necessary. In fact, a learner goes from being an amateur to an expert by acquiring a deep grasp of the theory. The difference is that with an applied-first approach, the theory emerges from and gets validated through practice, rather than preceding it.
